Overview

📋 Travel Considerations

We understand that many international travelers are apprehensive about entering the United States due to increased scrutiny at ports of entry and land crossings. If you need to travel, it is critical to carry the right documents for your visa classification and to be prepared for the possibility of additional screening or delays during reentry.

International travel is still permissible. Preparation is your best protection.

⚠️
Consult CIP Early

Travel guidelines and restrictions can change without notice. Reach out to Corporate Immigration Partners (CIP) and the Upstart Immigration Team well in advance of solidifying any travel plans — ideally with at least 30 calendar days' notice.

🌍 Employees with a Pending I-485 (Adjustment of Status)
🚨
Contact the Immigration Team immediately and well in advance of any travel if:
  • You are planning to travel using Advance Parole (AP)
  • Your passport is expiring soon
  • You have a pending Advance Parole renewal

Critical rules for Advance Parole travel:

  • You must have a valid, unexpired Advance Parole document in hand before you depart the U.S. Departing without a valid AP document will cause your pending AP application to be abandoned. Your I-485 and I-765 will remain pending, but without a valid AP you will have no immigration basis to re-enter the U.S.
  • If your AP expires while you are abroad, it cannot be used for re-entry. Contact CIP and the Immigration Team immediately if this occurs.
  • Carry all AP and I-485 supporting documentation when traveling.
  • AP travel may trigger additional scrutiny at reentry. Be prepared.

Consular Guidance

🏛 U.S. Consulate & Visa Stamping Tips

  • Make your appointment before leaving the U.S. Visit usembassy.state.gov to locate the appropriate U.S. Embassy or Consulate and follow their procedures for scheduling.
  • The consulate webpage will provide instructions on forms to complete, fees, photograph requirements, and other application requirements specific to your visa category.
  • Apply early. Allow time for PIMS verification and potential security clearance (221(g) administrative processing), which can add days or weeks.
  • Bring all original documents to your appointment.
  • Never surrender your original I-797 approval notice at a consulate appointment.
🚨
TCN Processing Restriction — Effective September 6, 2025

As of September 6, 2025, visa applications must be filed at a U.S. Embassy or Consulate in your country of nationality or residence. If you are abroad and need to apply at a third country consulate, contact CIP before scheduling — very limited emergency exceptions may apply at consular discretion and should not be relied upon.

At the Border

🛃 CBP Inspection Guidance

If there is any uncertainty at any point during the inspection process, reach out to CIP and/or the Upstart Immigration Team. Save both contact details in your phone before you travel.

ActionGuidance
Review your social media presence
  • It is recommended that you make all of your social media handles public before traveling. CBP may review public posts, and consular officers may request access during visa appointments (see DOS Updates).
  • Remove or archive anything that could be misread, including jokes or content implying unauthorized work, extended stays, political activity, or mixed travel intentions.
  • Be mindful that content related to criminal activity, drugs, or violence can lead to admission denials.
Double-check your job details before you go
  • If anything about your role may change (location, job duties, salary, title), loop in CIP and the Upstart Immigration Team before you travel.
  • We'll help determine if an amendment or other action is needed before you depart.
  • Reminder: USCIS must receive amendment petitions before material changes take effect.
Keep your CBP conversation focused
  • Answer questions truthfully and succinctly — no need to overshare.
  • Stick closely to what's reflected in your immigration paperwork, especially your job duties, role, and worksite(s).
Be ready to explain past immigration history
  • If you've had visa refusals, overstays, or prior CBP encounters, prepare a clear, simple explanation in advance.
  • Keep any supporting documentation readily accessible.
Know your rights regarding device searches
  • CBP has broad legal authority to search, copy, or detain electronic devices at the border without a warrant.
  • Before traveling: back up all data and consider removing sensitive or confidential Upstart information from your travel devices, or travel with a clean work device.
  • If CBP requests access to your device, you are generally required to comply. Refusal can result in device seizure or denial of entry.
  • Contact CIP or the Immigration Team immediately if you are asked to unlock a device or provide passwords at a port of entry.
Secondary Inspection

🔍 Secondary Inspection Guidance

Being selected for secondary inspection does not necessarily indicate a problem. This is a standard CBP procedure to verify immigration status, employment details, or travel history. It can take anywhere from 30 minutes to several hours. Stay patient, provide concise and truthful answers, and refrain from using electronic devices unless requested.

💡
You may ask to contact your immigration attorney or the Upstart Immigration Team at any point during secondary inspection. Save CIP and Upstart immigration contacts in your phone before you travel. If you are asked to sign any documents, request the opportunity to speak with an attorney first.

CBP officers may ask questions across the following categories. Familiarize yourself with these before travel:

General Information
  • What is the purpose of your visit to the United States?
  • How long do you plan to stay in the U.S.?
  • Where will you be staying? (Provide address if asked.)
  • Have you been to the U.S. before? If so, when and for how long?
  • Do you have family or friends in the U.S.? If so, who are they, and where do they live?
Immigration & Visa Status
  • What type of visa do you have?
  • What is your current immigration status? (H-1B, L-1, F-1, etc.)
  • Do you have your I-797, I-20 (if applicable), or other status documents?
  • Have you ever overstayed a visa or had any issues with U.S. immigration?
  • Have you ever been denied a U.S. visa or entry?
Employment & Work-Related (Work Visa Holders)
  • Who is your employer?
  • What is your job title and role?
  • Where is your work location?
  • How long have you worked for your employer?
  • Do you have an employment verification letter or recent pay stubs?
  • Are you currently on a valid payroll and receiving a salary?
Education-Related (F-1 or J-1 Visa Holders)
  • What school or program are you attending?
  • What is your major or field of study?
  • Are you currently enrolled as a full-time student?
  • Do you have your Form I-20 (F-1) or DS-2019 (J-1) with a valid travel signature?
  • Do you have proof of financial support for your studies?
Travel History & Background
  • Have you traveled outside the U.S. recently? If so, where and why?
  • Have you visited any countries subject to additional scrutiny?
  • Have you ever been arrested or convicted of a crime in any country?
  • Have you ever worked in the U.S. without authorization?
  • Are you carrying any prohibited items, including large amounts of cash or business-related materials?
Miscellaneous
  • Who purchased your flight ticket?
  • Are you traveling alone or with family?
  • Do you have a return ticket or onward travel plans?
  • Is there anyone you want to contact before continuing the inspection?
Policy Updates

🌐 Department of State Travel Updates (2025–2026)

⚠️
Verify Before Travel

This section reflects policy updates current as of June 30, 2026. These policies change frequently and without notice. Always verify current requirements with CIP before finalizing any travel plans.

In-Person Interview Requirement (Effective September 2, 2025)

In-person interviews for visa stamp applications are now required for most nonimmigrant visa categories. Online or waived interview processes that were previously available are largely no longer an option.

TCN Processing Restriction (Effective September 6, 2025)

As of September 6, 2025, visa applications must be filed at a U.S. Embassy or Consulate in your country of nationality or residence. Effective November 1, 2025, immigrant visa applicants must also interview in the consular district designated for their country of residence or nationality. If you are abroad and need to apply at a third country consulate, contact CIP before scheduling — very limited emergency exceptions may apply at consular discretion and should not be relied upon.

Enhanced Vetting for H-1B & H-4 Applicants (Effective December 3, 2025)

The U.S. Department of State expanded vetting requirements for H-1B visa applicants and their H-4 dependents, including additional document verification, heightened scrutiny of employment arrangements, and more detailed background checks.

Online Presence Reviews — H-1B, H-4, F, M, and J Applicants

Consular officers may request that applicants make their social media accounts public during visa interviews. It is recommended that you adjust your privacy settings to make your accounts publicly viewable before your visa appointment. This guidance expanded a June 27, 2025 policy to include H-1B applicants, H-4 dependents, and F, M, and J visa applicants.
